## Bottom Line

No peer-reviewed studies have administered Busemeyer-style quantum-cognition tasks (QQ equality, conjunction fallacy, disjunction effect) to epilepsy patients ictally, peri-ictally, or inter-ictally. The epilepsy decision-making literature uses Iowa Gambling Task, Game of Dice, and probabilistic gambling paradigms, consistently showing impaired decision-making under ambiguity and impaired feedback learning.

The seizure/anesthesia contrast depends on which criticality formalism is used. In the avalanche/branching literature, interictal epileptiform activity deviates from healthy criticality with higher neural gain and larger avalanches (compatible with supercritical reading). In the edge-of-chaos literature, generalized spike-wave seizures are modeled as hyper-stable/periodic, while GABAergic anesthesia is modeled as more chaotic/unstable. The current literature does not support a simple "seizures are supercritical and anesthesia is subcritical" across all levels of description.

## 1. Decision-Making and Probability-Judgment Signatures in Epilepsy

- 2018 systematic review/meta-analysis: moderate overall decision-making deficit in epilepsy, driven most robustly by poorer Iowa Gambling Task performance
- 2025 EEG/ERP study: both new-onset and seizure-remission epilepsy groups had lower IGT net scores, reduced P300 amplitudes, reduced theta power vs controls
- Temporal-lobe epilepsy: relatively selective impairment on ambiguity/feedback-based tasks rather than explicit-risk tasks
- 2022 comparison of TLE and FLE: worse decision-making under ambiguity in both groups than controls
- 2023/2022 work: impaired reward-based decision-making and statistical learning associated with seizure burden and hippocampal circuitry

**Gap:** No direct ictal/peri-ictal epilepsy test of quantum-probability judgment signatures exists.

## 2. Branching Ratio / Criticality: Seizures vs Anesthesia vs Wakefulness

**Anesthesia (clear):**
- 2024 *Communications Biology*: propofol and xenon reduced branching ratio, repertoire size/diversity, and avalanche-criticality measures → subcritical regime
- Defines criticality as branching ratio near 1, subcritical at <1
- Significant decreases under propofol and xenon, but not ketamine

**Epilepsy (formalism-dependent):**
- Avalanche framework (2016 J. Neurosci.): interictal epileptiform activity showed higher neural gain, larger avalanches, substantial deviations from critical benchmark; healthy controls near σ = 1 → "toward supercritical"
- Edge-of-chaos framework (Toker et al. 2022): generalized spike-wave seizures = hyper-stable/periodic; GABAergic anesthesia = chaotic/unstable
- Toker et al. 2024 (eLife): bidirectional cross-frequency corticothalamic information transfer decays as thalamocortical dynamics hyper-stabilize in seizures; anesthesia shows different, increasingly chaotic departure

**Summary:**
- Wakefulness: near critical / near optimal propagation
- Propofol/xenon anesthesia: subcritical (avalanche); chaotic/unstable (edge-of-chaos)
- Interictal epileptiform activity: higher gain/larger avalanches, compatible with supercritical
- Generalized spike-wave seizure: hyper-stable/periodic, not simple supercritical branching

## 3. Absence Seizures as a Cleaner Case

- Simultaneous EEG-fMRI-behavior: 81% omission-error rates on continuous performance task, 39% on simpler repetitive tapping during spike-wave discharges
- Seizures with impaired behavior showed larger EEG/fMRI amplitudes and were longer
- Video-EEG reading studies: longer absence seizures produce reading arrest with 1–3 s latency after onset, post-absence amnesia ~1–4.5 s
- Children with childhood absence epilepsy also have interictal deficits in attention, vigilance, impulsivity, reduced medial frontal activation

**Gap:** No ictal or peri-ictal absence-seizure study using conjunction-fallacy, order-effect, or QQ-equality paradigms.

## 4. Information-Theoretic Measures: Seizure vs Anesthetic States

- Anesthesia: propofol/xenon reduce Lempel-Ziv complexity, fractal dimension; linked to subcritical dynamics and lower perturbational complexity
- Absence seizure EEG (permutation entropy): entropy decreases from seizure-free → pre-seizure → seizure
- 2022 *Scientific Reports*: focal temporal-lobe seizure altered awareness associated with loss of signal complexity/information processing
- Partial/focal seizure work shows early local increases in certain complexity measures at seizure onset
- Toker's common framework: both propofol anesthesia and generalized spike-wave seizures reduce cross-frequency thalamocortical information transfer, but from different sides of the optimum
- No convincing ictal PCI literature analogous to anesthesia PCI literature

## 5. Loss of Adaptive Selectivity During Seizures

**Key finding for NFT — directly supports back-action thesis:**
- Rat model of absence epilepsy: sensory coding in epileptic onset zone was delayed and duplicated, indicating degraded intracortical processing even when overt behavioral discrimination preserved
- Human focal seizures: pathological discharges reorganize population spiking into seizure-specific temporal sequences diverging from baseline coding
- Epileptic mice: disrupted interneuron synchronization → unstable hippocampal place coding, lower information content, worse decoding of position/direction

Best formulation: seizure dynamics replace flexible, input-sensitive coding with stereotyped, seizure-dominated propagation patterns, reducing discriminability and stable information representation.

The literature is more interesting — and less cleanly supportive of your starting intuition — than I expected.

### Bottom line

I did **not** find peer-reviewed studies that administered **Busemeyer-style quantum-cognition tasks** such as the **QQ equality**, **conjunction fallacy**, or **disjunction-effect** paradigms to epilepsy patients ictally, peri-ictally, or inter-ictally. What I did find is a sizable epilepsy literature using **Iowa Gambling Task**, **Game of Dice**, **probabilistic gambling**, and **statistical-learning** paradigms, which consistently shows impaired decision-making under ambiguity and impaired feedback learning in several epilepsy populations.

The bigger theoretical wrinkle is this: the seizure/anesthesia contrast depends on **which criticality formalism** you use. In the **avalanche/branching** literature, interictal epileptiform activity deviates away from healthy criticality with **higher neural gain and larger avalanches**, which is compatible with a "supercritical-ish" reading. But in the newer **edge-of-chaos** literature, **generalized spike-wave seizures** are modeled as **hyper-stable/periodic**, while **GABAergic anesthesia** is modeled as **more chaotic/unstable**. So the current literature does **not** support a simple one-line slogan that "seizures are supercritical and anesthesia is subcritical" across all levels of description.

### 1. Decision-making and probability-judgment signatures in epilepsy

The direct "quantum cognition task" literature appears to be missing. The epilepsy decision-making literature I found is built around conventional decision tasks: a 2018 systematic review/meta-analysis reported a **moderate overall decision-making deficit** in epilepsy, driven most robustly by poorer **Iowa Gambling Task** performance. A 2025 EEG/ERP study found that both **new-onset** and **seizure-remission** epilepsy groups had lower Iowa Gambling net scores, reduced **P300** amplitudes, and reduced **theta** power relative to controls. Earlier temporal-lobe epilepsy work also found relatively selective impairment on ambiguity/feedback-based tasks rather than on explicit-risk tasks.

That means the strongest evidence right now is **not** "epilepsy changes QQ-equality violations"; it is "epilepsy often impairs ambiguity-based decision-making, feedback learning, and statistical learning." The 2022 comparison of temporal-lobe and frontal-lobe epilepsy found worse decision-making under ambiguity in both groups than in controls, and a 2023/2022 line of work showed impaired reward-based decision-making and impaired statistical learning associated with seizure burden and hippocampal circuitry.

So for your discriminative prediction, the honest state of play is: **there is currently no direct ictal/peri-ictal epilepsy test of quantum-probability judgment signatures**, but there is a good behavioral literature showing that epilepsy changes decision-making and learning in ways that could motivate such experiments.

### 2. Branching ratio / criticality: seizures vs anesthesia vs wakefulness

For anesthesia, the evidence is fairly clear. A 2024 *Communications Biology* study reported that **propofol** and **xenon** reduced branching ratio, repertoire size/diversity, and other avalanche-criticality measures, concluding that unconsciousness under those agents reflects a shift into the **subcritical** regime. The same paper explicitly defines criticality as branching ratio near **1**, with subcritical dynamics at **<1**, and reports significant decreases in branching-ratio-related measures under propofol and xenon, but not ketamine.

For epilepsy, the answer depends on state and formalism. In the **avalanche** framework, the 2016 J. Neurosci. study on **interictal epileptiform activity** found that epilepsy patients, especially during brief **interictal epileptiform activity (IEA)** periods, showed **higher neural gain**, **larger avalanches**, and substantial deviations from the critical benchmark; healthy controls and non-IEA periods were much closer to the critical expectation, including the theoretical reference **σ = 1**. That is the cleanest support I found for a "toward-supercritical" interpretation in epilepsy.

But the newer **edge-of-chaos** work points the other way for **generalized spike-wave seizures**. Toker et al. 2022 place waking consciousness near the top of an inverse-U of information richness, with **GABAergic anesthesia** on the **chaotic/unstable** side and **generalized seizures** on the **periodic/stable** side. Their 2024 eLife follow-up similarly reports that bidirectional cross-frequency corticothalamic information transfer decays most strongly as thalamocortical dynamics **hyper-stabilize** in generalized spike-wave seizures, whereas anesthesia shows a different, increasingly chaotic departure from the same optimum.

So the most defensible summary is:

* **Wakefulness:** near critical / near optimal propagation.
* **Propofol/xenon anesthesia:** subcritical in avalanche terms; chaotic/unstable in edge-of-chaos terms.
* **Interictal epileptiform activity:** higher gain/larger avalanches, compatible with supercritical deviation.
* **Generalized spike-wave seizure:** often modeled as hyper-stable/periodic rather than simple "supercritical branching."

That distinction matters a lot for NFT.

### 3. Absence seizures as a "cleaner" case

Absence seizures do give you a relatively clean behavioral-loss-of-consciousness model, but not yet in the probability-judgment sense you want. In simultaneous EEG-fMRI-behavior studies, childhood absence seizures produced major task-performance deficits: one Yale study reported omission-error rates of **81%** on a continuous performance task and **39%** on a simpler repetitive tapping task during spike-wave discharges. A later *Lancet Neurology* study found that absence seizures with impaired behavior showed larger EEG/fMRI amplitudes and were longer than seizures with spared behavior.

There are also more granular cognitive signatures. Video-EEG reading studies found that longer absence seizures produce **reading arrest** with **1–3 s latency** after seizure onset and **post-absence amnesia** lasting roughly **1–4.5 s**. Separate work shows that children with childhood absence epilepsy also have **interictal** deficits in attention, vigilance, impulsivity, and related prefrontal-network function, including reduced medial frontal activation and altered connectivity.

What I did **not** find was an ictal or peri-ictal absence-seizure study using conjunction-fallacy, order-effect, or QQ-equality paradigms. So absence seizures are "clean" mainly for **attention/arousal/performance** and network-state comparisons, not yet for **probability-judgment geometry**.

### 4. Information-theoretic measures: seizure states vs anesthetic states

There is meaningful overlap in metrics, but not much same-subject head-to-head work. For anesthesia, propofol and xenon reduce **Lempel–Ziv complexity** and fractal dimension while increasing some entropy measures; the 2024 anesthesia-criticality paper links this to subcritical dynamics and lower perturbational complexity.

For seizures, several entropy/complexity studies also report reduced complexity during altered-awareness states, but the pattern depends on seizure type and metric. Human absence-seizure EEG studies using **permutation entropy** found that entropy decreases from seizure-free to pre-seizure to seizure phases. A 2022 *Scientific Reports* study on focal temporal-lobe seizures reported that **altered awareness** was associated with a **loss of signal complexity/information processing** measured via permutation entropy. But partial/focal seizure work can also show **early local increases** in certain complexity measures at seizure onset, indicating that "seizure = lower complexity" is too crude without specifying metric, scale, and seizure stage.

The best common-framework comparison I found is Toker's work: both propofol anesthesia and generalized spike-wave seizures reduce **cross-frequency thalamocortical information transfer**, but they do so from **different sides** of the optimum — seizures by hyper-stabilization, anesthesia by increasing chaoticity. Separate transfer-entropy work in intracranial EEG also shows that seizure onset/offset alters directed information flow in specific ways, though this is not a consciousness comparison study.

I did **not** find a convincing ictal **PCI** literature analogous to the anesthesia PCI literature. So today's best empirical comparison is more like **LZ/entropy/edge-of-chaos/transfer-entropy** than PCI proper.

### 5. "Loss of adaptive selectivity" during seizures

Here the literature is actually pretty supportive of your intuition, though the mechanism is more nuanced than "everything responds to everything." In a rat model of absence epilepsy, sensory coding in the epileptic onset zone was **delayed and duplicated**, indicating degraded intracortical processing even when overt behavioral discrimination could be preserved. In human focal seizures, pathological discharges reorganize population spiking into seizure-specific temporal sequences that diverge from baseline coding patterns. In epileptic mice, disrupted interneuron synchronization produces unstable hippocampal place coding, lower information content, and worse decoding of position/direction.

So the cleanest formulation is not "the brain responds indiscriminately to everything," but:

> seizure dynamics can replace flexible, input-sensitive coding with stereotyped, seizure-dominated propagation patterns, reducing discriminability and stable information representation.

That is close to the selectivity-loss idea you want, but better supported.

### What this says for the NFT prediction

The current literature gives you **partial support** for the high-level discriminative idea that **anesthesia** and **seizure** are different failures of information processing, not the same thing with different causes. Anesthesia under propofol/xenon is consistently associated with reduced avalanche propagation, lower LZC, and subcritical or overly dissipative dynamics. Generalized spike-wave seizures also reduce awareness and cross-frequency information transfer, but in the edge-of-chaos literature they look **hyper-stable/periodic**, not simply "too much branching." Interictal epileptiform activity, by contrast, is the place where the more classical "supercritical-ish" avalanche picture shows up most clearly.

The weakest part of the program right now is exactly the one you asked about first: there is **no direct literature yet** testing whether epilepsy changes **quantum-cognition-style probability signatures**. If you want a decisive NFT-style discriminative test, that appears to be an open experimental niche rather than an already-answered question. The most defensible bridge from current evidence is: epilepsy measurably impairs ambiguity-based decision-making and information processing, and different unconscious states occupy different dynamical regimes, but the **QQ-equality / conjunction-fallacy** layer has not yet been mapped onto those regimes.
