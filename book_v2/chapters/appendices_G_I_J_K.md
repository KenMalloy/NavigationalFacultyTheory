### Appendix G: Extended Engagement with Competing Frameworks

Any theory of consciousness owes a debt to its competitors. Not a grudging one. If you can't articulate what rival frameworks get right, you haven't understood the problem well enough to propose a solution.

**The three-level evidential framework.** The main text organizes NFT's empirical commitments into three levels: substrate tests (does the proposed quantum effect exist in the relevant biology?), linkage tests (does perturbing that effect change conscious access?), and discriminative tests (is the observed pattern better explained by the quantum-linked model than by a classical alternative?). These are not consensus standards in the field. They are offered as a methodological scaffold, a way to make the claims honest and losable. A substrate result without linkage is physics, not consciousness science. A linkage result without a discriminative comparison is a correlation, not a mechanism.

The same framework applies to NFT's competitors. Any theory that cannot specify what would count against it is not a theory. It is a mood.

**Quantum cognition models.** Busemeyer and Pothos have developed a sophisticated program showing that quantum probability theory, the mathematics of superposition, interference, and incompatible observables, describes human decision-making patterns better than classical probability in several well-studied paradigms: conjunction fallacy violations, order effects in survey responses, the disjunction effect. Their work is careful, replicable, and does not require a quantum brain. The math is quantum. The substrate need not be.

NFT agrees with the math. The disagreement is about whether the fit is a coincidence. Quantum cognition treats quantum probability as a useful formalism, the way you might use complex numbers in electrical engineering without claiming the circuit is imaginary. NFT proposes that the formalism fits because the underlying substrate is, in fact, quantum. That is a stronger claim, and it requires stronger evidence. But if quantum cognition's modeling success were purely accidental, the pattern of where the fit succeeds and where it fails should be random. If NFT is right, the fit should track cases where the proposed biological substrate is most active. That is a testable difference.

**Recurrent Processing Theory.** Lamme's RPT holds that consciousness arises from recurrent neural processing, feedback loops in cortical circuits that integrate information over time. RPT has genuine empirical traction: recurrent processing correlates with reportable awareness in visual masking paradigms, and disrupting it disrupts conscious access.

NFT does not compete with RPT. It subsumes it. Recurrent processing is the neural architecture through which navigational selection operates. RPT answers a "where in the brain" question. NFT answers a "why does recurrent processing produce experience and feedforward processing doesn't?" question. The claim is that recurrent dynamics provide the feedback structure necessary for quantum measurement cycles to propagate and integrate. If recurrent processing were disrupted and consciousness survived, that would challenge both theories.

**GNWT after COGITATE.** Global Neuronal Workspace Theory, developed by Baars and elaborated by Dehaene and colleagues, proposes that consciousness corresponds to global broadcast: information becomes conscious when it is made available to multiple specialized brain modules simultaneously through workspace neurons with long-range connections. It is the most experimentally productive theory in the field.

The COGITATE adversarial collaboration, published in *Nature* in 2025, tested preregistered predictions of both GNWT and IIT in 256 participants using fMRI, MEG, and intracranial EEG. The results substantially challenged key predictions of both theories. GNWT was challenged by the lack of an ignition signature at stimulus offset and limited prefrontal representation of some conscious content dimensions. IIT was challenged by the absence of sustained synchronization in posterior cortex.

Neither theory was killed. Both were wounded in specific, documented ways. NFT reads this result as evidence that workspace broadcast and information integration are subsystems of something larger, not competitors for the whole explanation. That reading could be wrong. But it generates a prediction: the failures of GNWT and IIT should be complementary rather than overlapping, reflecting the fact that each theory captures a different piece of the navigational architecture.

**Hard criteria for any quantum consciousness theory.** I want to be direct about what any serious quantum consciousness proposal must satisfy, including this one. First: identify a specific quantum effect in the relevant biological substrate. Vague appeals to quantum weirdness do not count. Second: show that perturbing the quantum effect changes something about consciousness, not just neural activity in general. Third: demonstrate that the observed pattern cannot be equally well explained without the quantum mechanism. NFT currently clears the first criterion partially (radical pair feasibility modeling, not direct measurement), the second criterion not at all (no experiment yet connects radical pair perturbation to conscious access), and the third criterion in simulation only (navigation benchmarks show a modest quantum advantage in 3D/6D). A framework that honestly reports where its evidence ends is more trustworthy than one that pretends the evidence is complete.

### Appendix I: Philosophical Supplements

**The block universe in more detail.** The block universe sits at the intersection of two ideas that most physicists accept individually and find disturbing together. First: Einstein's field equations are time-symmetric. They work identically forward and backward. The laws of physics contain no variable for "now." Second: the relativity of simultaneity means that two events simultaneous for one observer are sequential for another moving at a different velocity. Putnam's argument follows: if my future is already your present, and your present is real, then my future is real too. All of it, already.

The tension with quantum mechanics is real and unresolved. Quantum measurement appears to introduce genuine indeterminacy, moments where the future is not yet fixed. If the block universe is correct and all events are equally real, what does it mean for a quantum measurement to have an undetermined outcome? Either the outcomes are all determined and we just don't know which one we'll experience, or the block universe picture is incomplete.

The growing block alternative, developed by Broad and refined by Tooley, offers a middle path. The past and present are real. The future is not, or not yet. Spacetime grows as events become actual, like a crystal accreting one layer at a time. This preserves the fixity of the past while allowing the future to be genuinely open. NFT's Level C is more naturally compatible with the growing block than with a strict eternalist block, because navigational selection requires that the alternatives being selected among are genuinely available, not already settled.

But I should be honest: the growing block has its own problems. It requires a privileged present, a "now" that marks the edge of reality, and special relativity provides no natural candidate for that boundary. The growing block trades one puzzle for another. NFT does not require settling this debate. The empirical program goes through regardless. Level C offers an engagement with the question, not a solution.

**Mary's room and the knowledge argument.** Frank Jackson's thought experiment: Mary is a brilliant neuroscientist who has learned everything physical there is to know about color vision, but she has lived her entire life in a black-and-white room. When she finally sees red for the first time, does she learn something new?

If she does, then physical knowledge is incomplete. There are facts about experience that cannot be captured by any amount of physical description. This is the knowledge argument against physicalism, and it remains one of the sharpest tools in the philosophy of mind.

NFT's response is specific. Mary's physical knowledge, however complete, was knowledge *about* the system. It was third-person, observational, descriptive. What she gains when she sees red is not additional information in the same category. It is the first-person disturbance that accompanies a measurement-like update of her own perceptual state. The back-action proposal from Chapter 12 suggests that qualitative character is tied to the physical disturbance intrinsic to a measurement-like process. Mary knew everything about color processing from the outside. She had never undergone the specific disturbance that constitutes seeing red from the inside.

This does not dissolve the hard problem. It relocates it. The question becomes: why does this particular disturbance produce this particular quality? That question remains open. But the relocation is not trivial. It narrows the search space from "something about computation" to "something about disturbance-bearing update processes," and that narrower target is, at least in principle, experimentally addressable.

**Causal closure and quantum indeterminacy.** The causal closure of physics says that every physical event has a sufficient physical cause. If consciousness causes anything, and consciousness is not physical, then causal closure is violated. This is the standard argument against dualism, and it is powerful.

Quantum indeterminacy does not provide a clean loophole. Here is why it looks like it might: if physical law determines the probabilities of outcomes but not the outcomes themselves, then there is causal slack. Something could influence which outcome occurs without violating any physical law. Consciousness could steer without breaking anything.

The problem is that filling indeterminacy with consciousness converts probabilistic physical law into deterministic psychophysical law, and now you need to explain why quantum statistics look random when they are actually being steered. NFT's approach is not to claim that consciousness violates or supplements physical law. It is to claim that the measurement-like process through which definite outcomes emerge is itself the locus of conscious experience. Consciousness does not push the dice. Consciousness is the rolling.

**The Godel analogy and its limits.** The constraint hypothesis from Chapter 2 draws an analogy to Godel's incompleteness theorems: a sufficiently complex formal system cannot prove all truths about itself from within. Consciousness science may face a structural version of this limit, where a conscious system cannot fully model its own consciousness because the modeling process is itself part of the system being modeled.

The analogy is precise in one respect: both involve self-reference generating principled limits on internal representation. A formal system that tries to encode its own consistency hits a wall. A conscious system that tries to model its own experience hits something similar, not because the information is hidden, but because the act of modeling changes what is being modeled.

Where the analogy breaks down: Godel's theorems apply to formal axiomatic systems with specific properties (consistency, sufficient expressiveness to encode arithmetic, recursively enumerable axioms). Brains are not formal systems. They are not consistent. They do not operate by syntactic proof. The incompleteness theorems do not apply literally to biological cognition. The constraint hypothesis uses Godel as a structural metaphor, not a theorem. The claim is that self-modeling limits are a feature of the territory, not a defect of current theory. But metaphors, however useful, are not proofs.

### Appendix J: Quantum Interpretations

NFT's empirical commitments are at Levels A and B. Level C, the ontological layer, touches quantum interpretation, and different interpretations receive the theory differently. What follows is an honest compatibility assessment. The theory should not be married to any single interpretation, and I want to show where each one creates friction.

**Copenhagen.** The standard textbook interpretation draws a line between quantum system and classical measuring apparatus. Measurement collapses the wavefunction. Before measurement, superposition. After, a definite outcome.

NFT fits naturally here. The proposal is that biological measurement-like processes, specifically radical pair chemistry in microtubules, constitute a physical instantiation of measurement. Consciousness is not an external observer collapsing wavefunctions from outside physics. It is the measurement process itself, instantiated in biological hardware. Copenhagen does not specify what counts as a measurement apparatus. NFT offers a candidate answer: any system with the right kind of integrated, disturbance-bearing update cycle.

**Many-worlds.** Everett's interpretation eliminates collapse entirely. Every quantum outcome is realized. The universe branches. All alternatives are equally real.

At Level A, this is fine. Navigation through possibility space maps onto the branching structure. The navigator follows one branch. From within that branch, the experience of selection is real even though no outcomes were discarded from the universe's perspective.

At Level B, the tension sharpens. NFT proposes that consciousness sculpts probabilities, biasing which outcomes are realized. In many-worlds, all outcomes are realized. There is nothing to sculpt. The question becomes: who selects which branch the navigator experiences? Many-worlds proponents answer that this is the wrong question, that the appearance of selection is an artifact of being located in a particular branch. But if probability sculpting is doing real computational work, as the navigation benchmarks suggest, then the mechanism needs somewhere to operate. Many-worlds makes the mechanism either redundant or incoherent, depending on how literally you take the branching. NFT at Level B sits uncomfortably here.

**Bohmian mechanics.** In Bohm's pilot wave theory, particles always have definite positions, guided by a wave function that never collapses. Quantum indeterminacy is epistemic, a reflection of our ignorance about initial conditions, not a feature of reality.

NFT is compatible with Bohmian mechanics, but the fit is awkward. The hidden variables provide the determinism that Level B does not need. If the radical pair outcomes are already determined by hidden variables, then consciousness is not selecting among genuine alternatives. It is following a script it cannot read. The navigational language survives as a description of the trajectory, but the agency drops out. You can have NFT's formalism in a Bohmian universe, but you lose the motivation.

**QBism.** Quantum Bayesianism, developed by Fuchs and Schack, treats quantum probabilities as an agent's personal degrees of belief. The wavefunction is not a physical object. It is a tool that a conscious agent uses to organize expectations about future experience.

This is the most natural home for NFT. Consciousness is the agent assigning probabilities. Possibility space is the agent's structured landscape of expectations. Measurement is the agent's interaction with the world, and the Born rule is a normative constraint on how a rational agent should update. QBism provides NFT with an interpretation where consciousness is not an afterthought bolted onto physics but the starting point from which physical description is organized.

The cost is ontological thinness. QBism is silent on what the world is like independent of agents. If you want possibility space to be physically real, as Level C proposes, QBism will not take you there. It gives you the agent. It does not give you the territory.

**Objective collapse (GRW/CSL).** Ghirardi, Rimini, and Weber proposed that wavefunctions collapse spontaneously, at random, without measurement. Continuous spontaneous localization (CSL) smooths this into a continuous process. Collapse is built into the dynamics.

NFT is partially compatible. If collapse parameters could depend on a consciousness-relevant variable, as Chalmers and McQueen have explored, then objective collapse provides a mechanism for consciousness-weighted actualization. But the parameter space is tightly constrained by interferometry, cryogenic force sensors, and gravitational wave detector data. Any consciousness-weighted variant must ensure that its parameters fall within the allowed region for non-conscious systems. This is demanding but not yet impossible. The surviving parameter corners are narrow.

**The early universe problem.** Every consciousness-linked interpretation of quantum mechanics must answer this: what collapsed the wavefunction before conscious observers existed? Stars formed. Heavy elements were synthesized in supernovae. Planets condensed from dust. All of this required definite physical outcomes, and none of it involved observers.

The standard replies are: consciousness was always present in some proto-form (panpsychism, which NFT does not endorse), the early universe evolved unitarily and only appeared definite retroactively (consistent histories, which is compatible but strained), or the question is malformed because "collapse" is not a physical event (QBism, many-worlds). NFT does not have a novel answer. It inherits whichever answer its chosen interpretation provides. This is not a unique weakness. Every interpretation-dependent claim about consciousness and measurement faces the same problem. But it is a real one, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest.

### Appendix K: Comparison with Stapp

Henry Stapp's quantum mind theory is the nearest ancestor to NFT's Level B, and the comparison deserves care. Stapp gets several things right. He also gets several things wrong in ways that are instructive.

**Shared commitments.** Both theories hold that quantum indeterminacy is not noise to be averaged away but the physical basis for genuine alternatives. Both hold that consciousness is intimately connected to the measurement process, the transition from quantum superposition to definite outcome. Both reject the view that consciousness is epiphenomenal, a helpless spectator watching a deterministic clockwork unfold. Both take the quantum Zeno effect seriously as a mechanism by which repeated observation stabilizes a physical state.

These are not small agreements. Most consciousness theories ignore quantum mechanics entirely or treat it as a metaphor. Stapp and NFT both insist that the physics matters, that the specific mechanism by which definite outcomes emerge from quantum superposition is where consciousness lives.

**Key difference: scale.** Stapp locates the relevant quantum process at the macroscopic neural scale, invoking von Neumann's Process 1 (the choice of what to measure) as an act of conscious attention that operates on brain-state superpositions. The entire brain, or at least large neural assemblies, exists in quantum superposition, and conscious attention selects which measurement is performed, thereby determining which brain state becomes actual.

NFT locates the process at the molecular scale. Radical pair chemistry in microtubule-associated proteins provides the quantum substrate. The superpositions are molecular, not neural. The measurement-like operations are local, rapid, and biologically specific. The macroscopic effects of consciousness emerge through amplification and integration of many molecular-scale events, not through brain-wide quantum coherence.

This difference matters enormously for the decoherence problem. Tegmark's calculation shows that macroscopic neural superpositions decohere in roughly 10^-13 to 10^-20 seconds, far too fast to be relevant to cognition. Stapp's theory must either refute Tegmark's assumptions or accept that the relevant superpositions are not neural-scale. NFT sidesteps this problem. Molecular radical pair states have coherence times on the order of microseconds, short by human standards, long enough for chemistry, and they are continuously regenerated. The decoherence objection does not apply to the same degree.

**Key difference: mechanism of volition.** Stapp uses the quantum Zeno effect as his primary mechanism for conscious will. Rapid, repeated observation of a neural state prevents it from evolving away from the intended configuration. Attention holds the brain state in place. The harder you concentrate, the more measurements you perform, the more stable the state.

NFT uses both Zeno and anti-Zeno effects, and for different purposes. Zeno dynamics stabilize perceptual states, holding a representation in place when sustained attention is needed. Anti-Zeno dynamics accelerate transitions, enabling flexible switching between states when the situation demands it. The ratio between Zeno and anti-Zeno regimes depends on the spectral density of the environment and the measurement rate, giving the theory a tunable parameter rather than a single mechanism. Attention is not just freezing. It is also releasing. The same physics does both, depending on the regime.

**Key difference: biological substrate.** This is where the gap is widest. Stapp's theory has no identified biological mechanism. Process 1 is described in the language of quantum measurement theory, not in the language of biochemistry. There is no specific molecule, no specific reaction, no specific cellular structure that instantiates the proposed quantum process. The theory operates at the level of formalism.

NFT proposes microtubule radical pairs as the substrate. This is specific enough to be wrong. Radical pair chemistry is well-characterized in other biological contexts (avian magnetoreception). Microtubules are ubiquitous in neurons and show measurable sensitivity to anesthetics. The spin coherence times, the chemical kinetics, the downstream coupling to protein conformation, all of these are individually measurable. The proposal may fail any of several empirical tests. That vulnerability is a feature.

**Relative strengths.** Stapp's theory is more philosophically developed. His engagement with von Neumann's measurement theory, with the role of the observer, and with the mind-body problem is sophisticated and sustained over decades of work. He takes the foundational questions seriously in a way that most neuroscientists do not.

NFT's strength is biological specificity. A theory that names its substrate, quantifies its parameters, and reports its negative results is closer to science than one that operates purely at the level of quantum formalism applied to neural-scale objects. Stapp gives you the philosophical architecture. NFT tries to fill it with chemistry.

**Assessment.** Stapp opened a door that most scientists preferred to keep closed: the possibility that quantum measurement and consciousness are not merely analogous but physically connected. That was an act of intellectual courage, and the field owes him for it. The limitation is that he never walked through the door far enough to find the biology. NFT attempts to walk further. Whether the biology it finds is the right biology remains an open experimental question. But the attempt to ground quantum consciousness in specific, testable, falsifiable molecular mechanisms is, I think, the right direction, even if the specific proposal turns out to be wrong.
